South Florida Market

The Most Culturally Complex Arena Market in North America

A market unlike any other in the NHL

Amerant Bank Arena sits in Sunrise, Florida — a city on the western edge of Broward County, equidistant between Fort Lauderdale and Miami. The arena draws from three counties: Broward (1.98M), Miami-Dade (2.76M), and Palm Beach (1.53M).[2] No other NHL market combines this level of Latin American influence, transient population, extreme wealth concentration, and year-round visitor activity in a single catchment zone.

Latin America's northern outpost

Broward County is 33% Hispanic/Latino. Miami-Dade is over 70% Hispanic/Latino.[2] Together, these counties represent the largest Cuban-American population in the United States and growing Venezuelan, Colombian, and Caribbean communities. The Cuban food tradition is not one influence among many in South Florida — it is the dominant culinary identity for millions of residents who grew up eating ropa vieja, croquetas, tostones, and café cubano as daily staples, not restaurant experiences.

The NHL fan paradox — confirmed and complicated

NHL fans nationally are 72% White and predominantly older (45% aged 55+).[5,6] Acxiom regional buyer data confirms the age skew — 47.8% of South Florida live event buyers are 55+ — but reveals a striking gender inversion: the regional buyer pool is 63% female, the direct opposite of the national NHL male majority.[19] This reflects the dominance of Latin music, cultural events, and family programming in the regional event mix. The implication is significant: any F&B program calibrated purely to a male-skewing hockey crowd is miscalibrated for the full event calendar this arena runs.

Two years of unprecedented growth

Back-to-back Stanley Cup championships in 2024 and 2025 transformed the Panthers from a struggling franchise (7,000 fans in 2014)[10] into the third-highest attended team in the NHL.[11] Ticket revenue climbed 50% in two years.[8] The team is now a genuine "destination franchise" — a growth market for F&B investment, not a mature one.

South Florida — Culinary Identity

Cuban Roots. Latin Soul. South Florida Table.

The culinary baseline

South Florida's food culture is anchored in Cuban and Caribbean tradition. Croquetas, cubanos, café cubano, tostones, ropa vieja, pastelitos, and black beans with rice are not "ethnic cuisine" to the majority of Broward and Miami-Dade residents — they are comfort food. Family staples. The default reference point. An operator who treats Cuban food as a specialty offering has fundamentally misread the market.

Beyond Cuban: the Latin mosaic

South Florida's Latin community is not monolithic. Colombian arepas, Venezuelan cachapas, Haitian griot, Jamaican jerk, Peruvian ceviche, Puerto Rican mofongo — these traditions have deep roots in the tri-county area. The arena event calendar increasingly programs for Latin audiences (Latin Billboard Awards, Uforia Mix Live, reggaeton headliners), and the F&B program must be capable of meeting these communities with genuine respect and fluency.

The northern transplant factor — confirmed by buyer data

South Florida has one of the highest in-migration rates in the U.S.[12] The Panthers' core fan base — older, educated, affluent — includes a significant population of transplants from the Northeast and Midwest who brought their hockey allegiances south. Acxiom regional buyer data confirms this directly: New York is the 5th largest feeder DMA in the dataset (1,701 buyers), behind only Miami–Ft. Lauderdale, West Palm Beach, Orlando, and Ft. Myers.[19] Chicago, Philadelphia, Boston, and Washington DC all appear in the top 15. These fans are acculturated to South Florida food culture but retain a premium dining sensibility from larger urban markets. They know the difference between a real Cuban sandwich and a facsimile.

Local restaurant competitive landscape

The Fort Lauderdale / Sunrise restaurant scene includes Cuban institutions like Las Vegas Cuban Cuisine (40+ years), Padrino's (50+ years), and scores of neighborhood cafeterias where café cubano and pan tostado are the default morning ritual. The hospitality benchmark at Amerant Bank Arena is set by this community standard — not by other NHL arenas.

South Florida — Restaurant Landscape

The Dining Scene That Sets the Arena's Bar

The Cuban institution tier — community anchors

A handful of restaurants define South Florida's Cuban culinary identity across generations. Padrino's Cuban Cuisine (50+ years, multiple Broward and Dade locations) remains the gold standard for slow-roasted pork, Cuban sandwiches, and event catering in the community. Las Vegas Cuban Cuisine in Sunrise — less than five miles from Amerant Bank Arena — has operated for 40+ years and is a literal neighbor. CAO Bakery & Cafe (Hialeah) and Versailles (Little Havana) anchor the cultural identity of Cuban food in South Florida's collective memory. These are not competitors to the arena — they are the standard against which arena food will be unconsciously judged by the Latin fans who eat at them regularly.

The neighborhood cafeteria tier — the everyday baseline

Across Broward and Miami-Dade, hundreds of ventanitas and Cuban cafeterias serve café cubano, croquetas, and pan con bistec at counter windows to morning commuters. This is not a restaurant experience — it is the default daily ritual for millions of South Florida residents. El Palacio de los Jugos (multiple locations) and countless neighborhood spots represent the cultural baseline. A venue that serves café cubano well earns extraordinary goodwill from these communities, because it signals genuine fluency, not menu checkbox.

The Fort Lauderdale premium tier — the corporate benchmark

The corporate suite client at Amerant Bank Arena benchmarks against the Fort Lauderdale and Miami premium restaurant scene, not other arenas. Louie Bossi's on Las Olas Boulevard is a Fort Lauderdale institution for Italian and craft cocktails. Steak 954 (W Fort Lauderdale) and ROK:BRGR represent the upscale casual category. In Miami, the reference points escalate: Zuma (Brickell), Stubborn Seed (South Beach), Casa Tua, and KYU (Wynwood) set the standard for the high-income corporate entertainer. When a suite client hosts a prospect at the arena, they are making an implicit statement about their taste level — and the food must hold up.

The emerging Latin fine dining tier — the rising benchmark

South Florida's most significant restaurant movement is the elevation of Latin cuisine into the fine dining tier. La Mar by Gastón Acurio (Mandarin Oriental Miami) puts Peruvian ceviches and tiraditos on a white-tablecloth stage. Cvi.che 105 (Downtown Miami) makes the same argument at a more accessible price point. In Wynwood, the modern Latin-fusion movement — Venezuelan, Colombian, and pan-Latin tasting menus — is establishing a new premium reference point. This matters for the arena because it means Latin food is no longer positioned below the premium tier in this market. An operator offering only a concourse croqueta kiosk while suites serve continental fare has badly misread the current landscape.

Strategic implication: local partnerships are the proof

In no other NHL market is the local restaurant scene so deeply intertwined with cultural identity and community trust. A bidder who names Padrino's or CAO Bakery as a concession partner signals market literacy that no national chain can replicate. A bidder who brings a La Mar-caliber Latin chef concept into the premium club tier signals that Latin cuisine belongs at the top of the menu, not the bottom. These partnerships are not vendor choices — they are cultural declarations, and this market's fans will read them accordingly.

Amerant Bank Arena + South Florida Live Event Market

A Building — and a Market — with Many Audiences

Panthers hockey is the spine of the building — but the event calendar serves a remarkably diverse set of audiences. Acxiom buyer data from 78,522 live event purchasers across tri-county South Florida venues confirms the scope and surfaces event categories not fully visible from census and NHL data alone. Each event type demands different F&B programming. A single menu strategy will fail them all.

Represents the same 7th-persona opportunity as the anime audience: a regular live event buyer who is not yet a Panthers fan but is already in the market.

Top Chef Competition Brief

Cooking for the 1%

The Florida Panthers RFP includes a live culinary competition: bidding operators will prepare and serve a seated dinner for the top 1% of Panthers season ticket holders. This is not an F&B operations review. It is a single-evening demonstration of culinary vision, South Florida cultural fluency, and premium hospitality execution — judged by the most discerning, most loyal, and most financially invested fans in the building.

The Audience — Top 1% Season Ticket Holders
$1M+
median home value bracket for this tier — Acxiom confirms 9% of regional buyers reach this threshold; top STH skew higher still[19]
NYC · BOS · DC
primary origin markets for the top STH transplant cohort[19] — these guests have eaten at Per Se, Le Bernardin, and Nobu. They know what premium is.
Ages 45–62
peak-earning, mid-to-late career, traveled widely — skeptical of performance without substance. Won't be wowed by price point alone. They live at that price point.[19]
Deep loyalty
back-to-back Cup champions — this group lived the run. Emotional investment in the Panthers brand is at an all-time high. The dinner must honor that.
What Winning Looks Like
The Winning Thesis
South Florida at its highest expression — not a national premium template dropped into a Miami zip code. The judges will see through a fine-dining performance that could happen anywhere. They want a meal that couldn't happen anywhere but here.
What Loses
Generic luxury. Wagyu for the sake of Wagyu. A sommelier who doesn't know the room. Plated food that looks like a hotel banquet. Price point alone will not impress people who live at that price point.
Top Chef Competition — Concept

"South Florida at the Table"

A four-course tasting menu structured as a culinary narrative of South Florida — from the sea to the land, from Cuba to the Keys. Every course sourced locally where possible. Every plate tells a specific story about where it came from and why it belongs here.

The Menu
Reception
Florida Stone Crab & Champagne
Chilled stone crab claws, Joe's-style mustard sauce, NV Billecart-Salmon Brut on arrival. Stone crab season runs October–May — perfectly aligned with the Panthers calendar. The opening statement is unmistakably South Florida.
Shared Table Course
South Florida Seafood Tower
Three tiers: Florida spiny lobster tail, jumbo Gulf shrimp, littleneck clams, oysters on the half shell, and additional stone crab claws — served over crushed ice with house cocktail sauce, mignonette, and a yuzu-habanero aioli. The centerpiece moment. Tableside presentation only — never pre-set. Paired: continued Billecart-Salmon or a dry Fino Sherry.
First Course

Day-boat Florida yellowtail, habanero-mango leche de tigre, crispy plantain chip, micro cilantro, finger lime pearls. The Caribbean and Latin DNA of South Florida in a single plate. Paired: Chablis Premier Cru.
Second Course

Florida spiny lobster, saffron, toasted sofrito foam, chive oil, warm brioche. The kitchen's technical moment — classic French bisque structure with a distinctly South Florida soul. Paired: white Burgundy or Viognier.
Latin Heritage Course
Ropa Vieja Short Rib + Pressed Cubano Bite
72-hour braised prime short rib in a sofrito-tomato braise with manzanilla olives, capers, and sweet peppers — the soul of Cuban home cooking at fine dining precision. Served with saffron congri, twice-fried maduros, and mojo verde. Accompanied tableside by a miniature pressed Cubano: mojo-braised heritage pork shoulder, house-cured ham, aged Swiss, house pickles on a laminated pan cubano — served hot off the plancha. The course that says this kitchen knows where it is. Paired: Rioja Reserva or Tempranillo.
Main Course
Surf & Turf — Black Grouper + A5 Wagyu
Pan-seared Florida black grouper, saffron-citrus beurre blanc, charred hearts of palm. Alongside: A5 Wagyu short rib, Cuban mojo glaze, sweet plantain purée, pickled red onion. Two proteins, two cultures, one South Florida statement. Paired: red Burgundy + Malbec.
Dessert
Key Lime — Elevated
Key lime tart, torched Italian meringue, Florida sea salt, vanilla bean gelato, candied lime zest. The unavoidable South Florida ending — executed with the precision it deserves. Paired: late-harvest Riesling or a Florida rum digestif.
Signature Cocktail
"The Sunrise"
Florida rum, fresh passionfruit, lime, champagne float, freeze-dried raspberry dust (Panthers red against gold). The opening cocktail that announces the evening — named for the team, worn like a second skin rather than a branded prop.
Ambiance & Theming
Space: Premium club or private suite converted to a chef's table environment — no arena signage, no plastic, no event lighting.
Design: Warm low light, linen tablecloths, full silver service, real stemware. Tropical botanical arrangements — birds of paradise, anthuriums — architectural, not kitschy.
Music: Live acoustic duo — Spanish guitar and soft vocals during reception, quiet jazz trio through dinner service.
Theater: Open kitchen view or tableside chef introduction at each course. Printed course cards with sourcing narrative accompany every plate.
Send-home: Local Florida honey, small-batch hot sauce, or a handwritten recipe card. The meal ends with a gesture, not a checkout.
The Legends Differentiator
Named local partner: Collaborate with a South Florida chef or institution — not as a vendor, but as co-author of the menu. A name with community equity no out-of-market operator can replicate.
Sourcing story: Every protein, produce item, and spirit carries a documented Florida provenance narrative. The judges are eating a map as much as a meal.
Sommelier present: Not a trained server pouring from a list — a wine director who can speak to every pairing decision and adapt in the room.
Synchronized service: Full brigade, all plates landed simultaneously. The technical standard is visible — and signals what the permanent program will look like at scale.
Zero plastic: Every touchpoint from welcome cocktail to digestif uses proper serviceware. The competition night is a promise about every night to come.
Bidder Strategy

Amerant Bank Arena: How to Win the Bid

The one thing that matters most
Latin cultural fluency is the price of admission. South Florida is the most Hispanic large metro in the U.S. — and Acxiom regional buyer data confirms that Latin music events are already the dominant event type by ticket volume at the market's venues.[19] The same data reveals a second non-negotiable: this market's live event buyer is 63% female — the inverse of the national NHL baseline. The operator who designs exclusively for a male hockey crowd has misread both the culture and the data.
✓   DO — Winning Themes
✓Lead with named South Florida Cuban and Latin restaurant partnerships — by name, with narrative. Padrino's, Las Vegas Cuban Cuisine, CAO Bakery & Cafe have 40+ years of community trust that money can't replicate.
✓Design explicitly for a female-primary buyer. Acxiom data shows 63% of regional live event buyers are female — the inverse of national NHL norms. Every F&B design decision should be tested against a female-first lens, not a default male hockey crowd.
✓Design an event-specific programming matrix — the Latin billboard audience and the Monster Jam family are not the same F&B brief. Show that granularity in your bid.
✓Build a serious Cuban and Caribbean concessions offering as core menu, not specialty kiosk: croquetas, cubano, tostones, café cubano, pastelitos. This is the everyday South Florida table.
✓Invest significantly in suite and club programming that matches South Florida's corporate and wealth tier. Acxiom confirms 42.5% of regional buyers own homes over $500K — the wealth concentration here is higher than prior estimates. The premium tier case should be pushed harder, not softer.
✓Build a craft cocktail program that includes mojitos, palomas, and coquito-inspired drinks as a permanent — not seasonal — menu feature. Latin cocktail culture is South Florida's cocktail culture.
✓Acknowledge the Panthers' growth trajectory in your bid narrative. Use the 50% revenue increase and back-to-back Cup data to frame a growing premium opportunity — not a flat-maintenance contract.
✓Name the Gen Z pop culture audience as a fan conversion opportunity. Acxiom data shows Otakufest and K-pop events rank among the highest-volume events in the region. These are not Panthers fans yet — but they are in the building, and a smart F&B + ticketing bundle plants the seed.
✗   AVOID — What Sinks a Bid
✗A national-chain-only concession program with no local South Florida culinary identity. This is the fastest path to cultural disqualification in this market.
✗Treating Latin food as a single-kiosk novelty addition to a "regular" menu. Cuban food is the regular menu for a large portion of this market — treat it as the anchor, not the outlier.
✗Calibrating all pricing to the affluent buyer profile without a clear value tier strategy. A meaningful portion of this market — particularly for Latin events and family shows — is mid-income or budget-constrained.
✗A wine and spirits program that doesn't include Cuban-inspired cocktails or Latin spirits (rum, cachaca, mezcal alongside premium bourbon). This is a significant miss in this specific market.
✗Benchmarking the premium program exclusively against other NHL arenas. The corporate and wealthy transplant fan base benchmarks against Miami and Fort Lauderdale's top restaurants.
✗One static suite menu for the full season. The corporate client entertaining quarterly will notice, and the Panthers' rising premium tier demands evolution.
✗Leading with your national footprint instead of your South Florida story. This venue wants a local partner, not a vendor that happens to have a Florida office.
Premium & Suite Strategy

The Premium Tier Is the Contract

The economic reality — confirmed by buyer data
Amerant Bank Arena has 74 private suites and 2,623 club seats.[1,14] The corporate South Florida market — Chewy, Carnival Corporation, AutoNation, Magic Leap, Citrix[9] — treats the arena as a client entertainment venue. Acxiom regional buyer data confirms the wealth concentration: 42.5% of buyers own homes worth over $500K, 9% over $1M, and 22% earn over $150K annually.[19] The premium tier case is stronger than prior estimates suggested. If your suite program wins this audience, you've won the contract's economic core.
✓   Premium Programming Wins
✓Name a Miami or Fort Lauderdale restaurant partner for premium suite catering — one with name recognition among the corporate and wealth class (Stubborn Seed, Zuma, Dune by Laurent Tourondel)
✓Sommelier-curated wine list with South American and Spanish wines prominently featured — culturally resonant and premium-tier credible in a Latin-influenced market
✓Seasonal suite menu refreshes tied to Panthers milestones — playoff push, home opener, Cup anniversary. Static menus signal coasting to an audience that returns quarterly.
✓Premium rum program as a South Florida signature — Zacapa 23, Diplomatico Reserva, Bacardi Gran Reserva. This has no equivalent in any other NHL market and creates genuine arena identity.
✓Pre-event dinner package tied to suite access — the South Florida corporate client expects a full evening experience, not just game-time catering
✗   Premium Programming Mistakes
✗A domestic light beer-forward bar program in suites. This audience has moved well past it — premium craft, imported lager, and Latin spirits are expected at this income tier in South Florida.
✗Suite menus that read like hotel banquet packages. The suite-holder knows every top restaurant in the market — match their reference point, not a catering catalog.
✗Ignoring the Latin entertainment events in suite programming. When the Latin Billboard Awards fill the building, the suite clients expect the food to reflect the moment.
✗Underinvesting in non-alcoholic premium options. South Florida's affluent Latin community includes significant segments that observe moderation — sophisticated mocktail programming matters.
✗No loyalty or tenure recognition for long-term suite and season-ticket holders. This audience has been patient through lean years — acknowledge their history with the franchise.
Key Takeaways

What Every Hospitality Bidder Must Know

01
South Florida — confirmed by data

Latin cultural fluency is not an add-on — it's the operational baseline. Acxiom buyer data shows Latin music events already dominate regional venue ticket volume, with Spanish-language artists occupying most of the top-20 events by buyer count.[19] Croquetas and café cubano belong in the base menu, not a specialty kiosk. This is not a future bet. It is the current reality.

02
Florida Panthers — wealthier than estimated

The premium tier is the contract — and it's bigger than the original analysis suggested. Acxiom data shows 42.5% of regional buyers own homes worth $500K+, and 9% own homes worth over $1M.[19] This audience benchmarks against Miami and Fort Lauderdale's best restaurants. The suite program investment case should be calibrated upward, not to prior estimates.

03
Gender — the biggest surprise in the data

The regional live event buyer is 63% female — the direct inverse of the national NHL male majority.[19] Every F&B design decision — concession format, beverage program, experiential programming, premium club design — should be tested against a female-primary buyer lens. This is the single largest recalibration the Acxiom data demands.

04
Event programming — and a new audience

Event-specific F&B is a competitive differentiator — and Acxiom data surfaces a seventh audience not in the original analysis: the Gen Z pop culture buyer (Otakufest, K-pop events rank among the top events by volume).[19] These are not Panthers fans yet. The operator who acknowledges them — with a mobile-first, value-forward, visually driven F&B offer — is making a 10-year fan development argument in their bid.
